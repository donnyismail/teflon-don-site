# 1. THE SALES FUNNEL

## The Pipeline at a Glance

```
COLD EMAIL (29 sent)
    |
    v  Target: 20-30% open rate, 5-10% reply rate
REPLY / WARM LEAD
    |
    v  Target: 70% of replies book a call
DISCOVERY CALL (15-20 min)
    |
    v  Target: 60% move to mockup stage
FREE MOCKUP DELIVERY (48-hour turnaround)
    |
    v  Target: 70% of mockup recipients take proposal call
PROPOSAL PRESENTATION (20-30 min)
    |
    v  Target: 50% close
CLOSED DEAL --> ONBOARDING --> UPSELL --> REFERRAL
```

## Stage-by-Stage Breakdown

### Stage 1: Cold Email (You Are Here)
- **What happened:** You sent 29 personalized cold emails to Houston Heights businesses.
- **Goal of this stage:** Get a reply. Any reply. Even "not interested" is a door you can work with.
- **Target conversion:** 2-3 replies from 29 emails (7-10%).
- **Your job:** Do NOT sell in the email. Sell the conversation. The only CTA is "Can I show you something?"
- **Timeline:** If no reply in 3 days, follow-up sequence begins.

### Stage 2: Reply / Warm Lead
- **What happened:** They replied. They showed a pulse. This is gold.
- **Goal of this stage:** Get them on a phone call or video chat within 48 hours of their reply.
- **Target conversion:** 70% of replies should become booked calls.
- **Your job:** Respond within 15 minutes during business hours. Speed wins deals. The faster you respond, the higher your close rate. After 30 minutes, your odds drop by 50%.
- **What to say when they reply:**

> "Hey [Name]! Really appreciate you getting back to me. I put together some ideas
> specifically for [Business Name] that I think you'll get a kick out of. Easiest
> thing would be to hop on a quick 15-minute call so I can walk you through what
> I'm seeing. Would tomorrow at [TIME] or [TIME] work for you? Either way, no
> pressure at all."

- **Key principle:** Always give two specific time options. Never say "when are you free?" That puts the burden on them and deals die in scheduling limbo.

### Stage 3: Discovery Call
- **What happened:** They're on the phone with you. This is where deals are won or lost.
- **Goal of this stage:** Understand their pain, qualify them, and get permission to build a free mockup.
- **Target conversion:** 60% of calls move to mockup stage.
- **Duration:** 15-20 minutes. Do NOT go over 25. Respect their time and they'll respect yours.
- **Your job:** Listen 70%, talk 30%. The person asking questions controls the conversation.
- **Full script in Section 2 below.**

### Stage 4: Free Mockup Delivery
- **What happened:** You told them you'd build something. Now you deliver.
- **Goal of this stage:** Blow their mind. Make them feel like you already built their new site.
- **Target conversion:** 70% of mockup recipients agree to a proposal call.
- **Turnaround time:** 48 hours MAX. Under-promise, over-deliver. Tell them 3-4 days, deliver in 2.
- **What to build:**
  - Homepage mockup (desktop + mobile screenshot)
  - Their logo, their colors, their actual business info
  - A "before and after" comparison (screenshot their current site next to your mockup)
  - One specific improvement they mentioned on the discovery call
- **How to deliver it:**

> Subject: Your new site is ready to preview, [Name]
>
> Hey [Name],
>
> I got excited after our call and went ahead and put something together for
> [Business Name]. I wanted you to see what's possible before we even talk numbers.
>
> Here's a quick preview: [LINK or ATTACHED IMAGE]
>
> Left side is your current site. Right side is what I built for you. The difference
> in how customers experience your business is night and day.
>
> A few things I included based on what you told me:
> - [Specific thing they mentioned, e.g., "Online booking so you stop playing phone tag"]
> - [Specific thing, e.g., "Mobile-first design since 70% of your customers are on phones"]
> - [Specific thing, e.g., "Google Maps integration so people actually find you"]
>
> Want to hop on a quick call so I can walk you through everything and talk about
> making this live? I've got [TIME] and [TIME] open tomorrow.
>
> -- Donny

### Stage 5: Proposal Presentation
- **What happened:** They've seen the mockup. They're interested. Now you present the deal.
- **Goal of this stage:** Close. Get a signed agreement and a deposit today.
- **Target conversion:** 50% of proposal calls close.
- **Duration:** 20-30 minutes.
- **Your job:** Walk them through the proposal (see Section 8), tie everything back to their pain points, and ask for the sale.
- **Key rule:** NEVER send a proposal over email without presenting it live first. Proposals sent cold have a 10% close rate. Proposals presented live have a 50%+ close rate.

### Stage 6: Closed Deal --> Onboarding
- **What happened:** They said yes. Congratulations. Now the real work starts.
- **Goal of this stage:** Collect deposit, set expectations, and start delivering.
- **Immediate actions after close:**
  1. Send invoice for 50% deposit via Stripe/PayPal/Venmo (whatever they prefer)
  2. Send welcome email with onboarding questionnaire (content, photos, logins)
  3. Set clear timeline: "You'll see the first full draft in [X] days"
  4. Add to your CRM/spreadsheet with status "Active Project"
  5. Schedule a 1-week check-in call

# 2. DISCOVERY CALL SCRIPT

## Pre-Call Prep (5 minutes before)

Before you pick up the phone or hop on Zoom, review:
- Their current website (screenshot it, note 3 specific problems)
- Their Google Business profile (reviews, photos, hours accuracy)
- Their social media (last post date, follower count)
- What they said in their email reply
- Their competitors' websites (find one that looks great)

Write down 3 specific things you noticed. You'll use these to build credibility in the first 60 seconds.

---

## THE SCRIPT

### OPENING (First 30 Seconds)

The goal: Break the ice. Sound human. Establish that this is a conversation, not a pitch.

> "Hey [Name], it's Donny over at Teflon Don. Thanks for taking the time, I know
> you're busy running [Business Name] so I'll keep this tight. How's your day going
> so far?"

[Let them answer. React naturally. 10 seconds of small talk max.]

> "Awesome. So like I mentioned in my email, I've been doing some work with
> businesses in the Heights and I came across [Business Name]. I actually really
> like what you guys are doing with [something specific -- their food, their
> service, their vibe, whatever]. I just think there's a huge opportunity to get
> more people through your door with a few tweaks online. But before I get into
> any of that -- I'd love to just hear from you. What's working for you right now
> when it comes to getting new customers?"

**Why this works:** You complimented them genuinely. You positioned yourself as someone who already knows their business. And you asked an open-ended question that gets them talking. The person talking feels heard. The person listening gathers intelligence.

---

### QUALIFYING QUESTIONS (Minutes 2-5)

The goal: Figure out if this is a real deal or a tire-kicker. You need to know three things: Do they have a problem? Can they pay? Can they decide?

**Question 1: The Pain**

> "When someone searches for [their service] in Houston Heights on Google, where
> are you showing up right now? Do you know?"

[Most won't know. That's the point.]

> "Got it. And how are most of your customers finding you right now -- is it mostly
> word of mouth, social media, walk-ins...?"

**Question 2: The Budget**

DO NOT ask "What's your budget?" directly. That scares people. Instead:

> "Have you guys invested in any marketing or web stuff before? Like, have you
> worked with anyone on a website or run any ads?"

[If yes:] "How'd that go? What did you spend on that?"
[If no:] "Totally fair. A lot of Heights businesses are in the same boat. You've been
so focused on running the business that the online piece just hasn't gotten the
attention it deserves. That's exactly where I come in."

**Question 3: The Decision Maker**

> "If we end up putting something together that makes sense for [Business Name],
> is that something you'd be able to pull the trigger on? Or is there a business
> partner or spouse you'd need to loop in?"

[If there's a partner:] "Totally get it. Would it make sense to have them on our
next call so they can see everything firsthand? That way you're not stuck playing
middleman."

---

### PAIN DISCOVERY (Minutes 5-10)

The goal: Make them feel the pain of their current situation. You're not creating pain -- you're helping them recognize pain they're already living with.

> "Let me ask you something, [Name]. When was the last time you looked at your
> website on your phone?"

[Pause. Let them answer.]

> "Here's why I ask -- I pulled up [Business Name] on my phone before this call,
> and honestly? A customer seeing this for the first time might not get the full
> picture of how great your business actually is. And right now, about 75% of
> people searching for local businesses are doing it from their phone. So that
> first impression matters a LOT."

> "And I looked at your Google presence too. You've got [X] reviews, which is
> [good/a start], but here's what I noticed -- when I search '[their service]
> Houston Heights,' your competitors are showing up above you. [Competitor Name]
> has a clean site, they're collecting reviews, they're running the playbook. And
> I don't think they're better than you -- I just think they're more visible."

> "Can I be real with you for a second, [Name]?"

[They'll say yes.]

> "You're leaving money on the table. Every day that someone searches for
> [their service] in the Heights and finds your competitor instead of you, that's
> a customer you should've had. And it's not because your [food/service/product]
> isn't great -- it's because your online presence isn't doing its job. Does that
> resonate with you?"

**Why this works:** You showed specific evidence. You used a competitor as social proof. You asked for permission to be direct. And you ended with a question that makes them verbalize their agreement.

---

### PRESENTING THE SOLUTION (Minutes 10-15)

The goal: Paint the picture of what life looks like AFTER they work with you. Don't sell features. Sell the outcome.

> "So here's what I'm thinking. I'd love to put together a complete online
> presence for [Business Name] that actually works as hard as you do. And I don't
> mean just a pretty website -- I mean a system that brings in new customers."

> "Specifically, here's what I'd build for you:"

> "First -- a new website that looks incredible on every device, loads fast, and
> is built to convert visitors into customers. Not just a brochure site. I'm
> talking click-to-call buttons, online booking, a menu/service page that actually
> sells, and built-in SEO so Google starts sending you free traffic."

> "Second -- I'd optimize your Google Business profile so you start showing up in
> the map pack when people search for [service] near them. That's the box with
> the three businesses that shows up at the top of Google. That's prime real estate,
> and right now [competitor] is sitting there instead of you."

> "Third -- and this is where it gets fun -- I can set up some AI automation for
> you. Things like automatic review requests after every customer visit, a chatbot
> on your site that answers questions 24/7, and automated follow-ups so no lead
> falls through the cracks."

> "The end result? More people find you. More people contact you. More people
> walk through your door. And you don't have to think about any of the tech --
> that's my job."

[Pause.]

> "How does that sound to you so far?"

---

### HANDLING THE PRICE QUESTION (When It Comes Up)

When they ask "How much does this cost?" -- and they will:

> "Great question. So my website packages start at $499 for a clean, professional
> site that's going to look light-years better than what you have now. If you want
> the full SEO buildout and some of the AI automation tools, that's in the $999
> to $2,499 range depending on how aggressive you want to be."

> "But honestly, [Name], here's how I think about it. If your average customer is
> worth -- what would you say? What's a typical ticket for you guys?"

[Let them answer. Example: "$50 per visit."]

> "Okay, so $50 a visit. And a good customer comes back, what, once a month? So
> that's $600 a year from one customer. If your new website and Google presence
> bring in just ONE extra customer a month -- and I'm being super conservative
> here -- that's $7,200 in new revenue over the next year from a $499 investment.
> That's a 14x return. And realistically, we're talking way more than one extra
> customer a month."

> "The website pays for itself in the first month or two. Everything after that
> is profit you wouldn't have had."

---

### CLOSING THE CALL (Last 2 Minutes)

The goal: Lock in the next step. Never end a call without a clear next action.

**If they're warm (interested, asking questions, engaged):**

> "Tell you what, [Name]. I'm going to do something I don't normally do. I'm going
> to put together a custom mockup of what your new website would look like --
> completely free, no obligation. I'll use your actual branding, your photos, your
> info. That way you can see exactly what you'd be getting instead of just imagining
> it. I'll have it ready for you by [DAY -- 2 days out]. Can we block 15 minutes
> on [DAY] to go over it together?"

**If they're lukewarm (noncommittal, "let me think about it"):**

> "I totally get it, and I never want anyone to feel rushed. Here's what I'd love
> to do though -- let me put together a quick side-by-side comparison of where you
> are right now versus where I think you could be. No cost, no commitment, I just
> want to show you what's possible. If you see it and think 'meh, not for me' --
> no hard feelings at all. Fair enough?"

**If they're cold (not interested, too busy):**

> "I hear you, and I respect your time. Can I just send you one thing? A quick
> before-and-after mockup so you can see it whenever you get a minute. If it gets
> you excited, great, we'll talk. If not, no worries at all. What's the best email
> for you?"

**After booking the next step:**

> "Awesome, [Name]. I'm really looking forward to showing you what I've got in
> mind for [Business Name]. You're going to love it. I'll shoot you a calendar
> invite right after we hang up. Talk soon!"

[Send the calendar invite within 5 minutes. Include a Zoom link. Add a note:
"Looking forward to showing you the mockup for [Business Name]!"]

# 4. OBJECTION HANDLING

## The Framework: Feel, Felt, Found + Reframe

Every objection gets the same structure:
1. **Acknowledge** (make them feel heard)
2. **Relate** (others have felt the same way)
3. **Reframe** (here's what they found / here's the truth)
4. **Redirect** (back to the close)

---

### Objection 1: "I can't afford it right now."

> "I totally hear you, [Name], and I'd never want you to stretch yourself in a way
> that doesn't make sense. Can I reframe this for a second though?
>
> Right now, you're paying the cost of NOT having this. Every day that your website
> isn't converting visitors into customers, that's money walking out the door to
> your competitors. A customer that finds [Competitor] instead of you -- that's
> $50, $100, however much your average ticket is -- gone.
>
> Over a year, that adds up to thousands of dollars in lost revenue. The $499 isn't
> a cost -- it's the thing that STOPS the bleeding.
>
> And here's what I can do: I can split it into two payments -- $250 now, $249 when
> the site goes live. That way you're not eating it all at once. Does that help?"

**Key move:** Always have a payment plan ready. It takes "I can't afford it" off the table for 90% of prospects.

---

### Objection 2: "I need to think about it."

> "Of course, and I'd never want you to rush into something you're not 100% on.
> Can I ask you though -- what specifically do you want to think about? Is it the
> price, the timing, or something about the plan itself?
>
> I ask because a lot of times I can clear up whatever's on your mind right now,
> and then you'll have all the info you need to make the best decision. What's the
> main thing you're chewing on?"

**Key move:** "I need to think about it" is never the real objection. It's a smokescreen. Your job is to uncover what's actually behind it. Once you ask "what specifically," they'll tell you the real concern, and you can handle that.

---

### Objection 3: "My nephew/cousin/friend can build me a website."

> "Ha, I hear that one a lot, and I love that you have family who's willing to help.
> Here's what I've seen happen though, and I say this with zero disrespect to your
> nephew -- building a website and building a website that actually brings in
> customers are two completely different things.
>
> Your nephew can probably make something that looks decent on a screen. But does he
> know how to structure it so Google ranks you for '[service] Houston Heights'? Does
> he know how to set up conversion tracking? Speed optimization? Mobile-first design
> that loads in under 2 seconds? Schema markup so you show up in rich results?
>
> This is what I do every single day for businesses like yours. It's my craft. And
> honestly, the businesses I've taken over from the nephew-built sites? Those are some
> of my biggest success stories, because the before-and-after is so dramatic.
>
> What if we do this -- let your nephew take a crack at it, and if it's not getting
> you the results you want in 90 days, give me a call. I'll still be here. But I'd
> hate for you to lose 3 months of potential customers in the meantime. Does that
> make sense?"

**Key move:** Don't trash the nephew. Respect the relationship. Differentiate on expertise and results, not design.

---

### Objection 4: "I already have a website."

> "You do, and I actually looked at it before our call. Can I share an honest
> observation?
>
> Your site [pick the most relevant]:
> - Takes [X] seconds to load on mobile (anything over 3 seconds and 53% of visitors bounce)
> - Isn't optimized for Google, so you're invisible for '[service] Houston Heights'
> - Doesn't have clear calls-to-action (no click-to-call, no online booking, no easy way to contact you)
> - Looks like it was built [whenever] and hasn't been updated since
>
> Having a website and having a website that works for you are two different things.
> Think of it this way -- you have a storefront on [street], right? That storefront
> has signage, it's clean, it draws people in. Your website is your digital storefront,
> and right now? It's like having a faded sign with the lights off. The people driving
> by online don't even know you're open.
>
> I'm not talking about scrapping what you have. I'm talking about giving you a website
> that actually earns its keep. Does that make sense?"

---

### Objection 5: "I don't need a website. I get all my business from referrals."

> "That's awesome, [Name], and it tells me you're delivering a great experience --
> people don't refer businesses they don't love. So congratulations on that.
>
> But let me ask you this: what happens when someone gets referred to you? What's
> the first thing they do?"

[Let them answer. If they don't know:]

> "They Google you. Every single time. And what they see when they Google you
> determines whether that referral becomes a customer or goes to someone else.
>
> I've got a stat that might surprise you -- 87% of consumers search online before
> visiting a local business, even if they were referred by a friend. If they Google
> [Business Name] and find an outdated website or no website at all, you just lost
> a warm lead that was practically yours.
>
> A website doesn't replace referrals. It closes the referrals you're already
> getting. It's the difference between converting 50% of your referrals and
> converting 90%. How many referrals do you think you're losing because your
> online presence doesn't match how good your actual business is?"

---

### Objection 6: "What if it doesn't work?"

> "That's a fair question, and I'd be skeptical too if I were in your shoes. Here's
> what I can tell you:
>
> First, I don't disappear after the site goes live. I'm not a build-it-and-bounce
> guy. I stick around, I track the performance, and I make adjustments. If something
> isn't working, I fix it.
>
> Second, I can point you to [X] other businesses I've worked with that had the
> same concern and are now seeing real results. Happy to connect you with [Name]
> at [Business] if you want to hear it straight from them.
>
> But here's the thing, [Name] -- I can't guarantee a specific number of new
> customers, because I don't control whether someone walks through your door. What
> I CAN guarantee is that your online presence will be dramatically better than
> it is today. More visible on Google. More professional when people find you.
> More likely to turn a visitor into a customer. And I'll keep working with you
> until we get there.
>
> Can I ask you this -- what does 'working' look like for you? What result would
> make you feel like this was worth every penny?"

[Whatever they say, write it down. That becomes your benchmark and your close: "So if I can get you [their definition of success], would you be ready to move forward?"]

---

### Objection 7: "I tried SEO before and it didn't work."

> "Ugh, I'm sorry to hear that, and unfortunately it's not uncommon. The SEO
> industry is full of people who take your money and send you confusing reports
> that don't translate to real business.
>
> Can I ask -- what did they actually do for you? And how long did you give it?"

[Listen. Most of the time it was either a scam, a low-effort service, or they quit after 2 months.]

> "Here's the thing about SEO -- it's real, and it works, but it has to be done
> right and you have to give it enough runway. Most businesses see meaningful
> results in 3-6 months when the strategy is solid.
>
> What I do differently is I start with the stuff that gets you results fastest --
> Google Business Profile optimization, local citations, and on-page SEO. These
> aren't vague 'we'll build links' promises. These are specific, measurable actions
> with specific, measurable results.
>
> And I'll show you exactly what I'm doing every step of the way. No black box.
> No confusing reports. Just 'here's what I did this month, here's how it moved
> the needle, here's what's next.' Total transparency.
>
> Would you be open to giving it another shot with someone who actually shows you
> the work?"

---

### Objection 8: "Can you do it cheaper?"

> "I appreciate you being upfront about that. Let me explain the pricing for a
> second and then we can talk about options.
>
> The reason I'm at $[price] is because I don't cut corners. You're getting a
> custom-designed site -- not a template I slapped your logo on. You're getting
> SEO baked in from day one. You're getting mobile optimization, speed optimization,
> and a site that's built to actually generate business. If I went cheaper, I'd
> have to start cutting those things, and that's the stuff that makes the
> difference between a website that sits there and a website that makes you money.
>
> That said, here are two things I can do:
>
> 1. I can split the payments to make it easier on cash flow -- $[half] now and
>    $[half] when we launch.
>
> 2. If budget is really tight right now, I can start with the essential package
>    at $499 and we can add the advanced features later as the site starts paying
>    for itself. You'll still get a massive upgrade from what you have now.
>
> Which of those sounds better for your situation?"

**Key move:** Never just drop your price. Either split payments or adjust scope. Dropping price tells them your original price was inflated.

---

### Objection 9: "I need to talk to my partner/spouse."

> "Of course, that's smart -- big decisions should be made together. What I'd love
> to do is set up a quick call with both of you so I can walk [Partner Name] through
> everything and answer any questions they might have. That way you're not stuck
> trying to sell it for me, and they get to hear it directly. Does that work?
>
> I could do [TIME] or [TIME] this week. What works best for both of you?"

**Key move:** ALWAYS try to get on a call with the decision maker. If you let your prospect "go explain it to their partner," the deal dies 80% of the time because they can't articulate the value as well as you can.

**If they insist on talking to the partner alone:**

> "Totally understand. Let me put together a one-page summary you can share with
> them -- the mockup, the pricing, and the key benefits. That way they're not
> going in blind. And I'll follow up with you on [specific day]. Sound good?"

---

### Objection 10: "I'll get back to you."

> "Sounds good, [Name]. Before we hop off though -- can I ask you something honest?
> On a scale of 1 to 10, with 10 being 'let's do this tomorrow,' where are you
> sitting right now?"

[If they say 7+:]

> "Okay, so you're feeling pretty good about it. What's the one thing that would
> get you from a [their number] to a 10?"

[Handle whatever they say.]

[If they say 4-6:]

> "Got it. What's holding you back? I'd rather know now so I can address it than
> have it be the reason this never happens."

[If they say 1-3:]

> "I appreciate your honesty. Sounds like this might not be the right fit or the
> right time. Is it the timing, or is it the offer itself? Because if it's timing,
> I'm happy to circle back in a month or two. And if it's the offer, I'd love to
> know what would make it a no-brainer for you."

**Key move:** The 1-10 scale forces them to commit to a number, which gives you something concrete to work with. "I'll get back to you" is the vaguest objection -- this technique makes it specific.

# 5. CLOSING TECHNIQUES

## Close #1: The Assumptive Close

**When to use:** When the prospect is clearly interested, asking questions about features and timelines, and hasn't raised serious objections.

**How it works:** Stop asking IF they want to buy and start asking HOW they want to buy.

> "So here's what I'm thinking for the timeline, [Name]. If we kick this off today,
> I can have your first draft ready by [DATE -- 7-10 days out]. From there, we'll
> do a round of revisions, and you should be live within 2-3 weeks. For the deposit,
> do you prefer to do Venmo, PayPal, or credit card?"

**Why it works:** You skipped the "do you want to do this?" step entirely. You went straight to logistics. Most people will follow your lead and start talking about payment methods instead of questioning whether they should buy.

**If they push back:** "Oh, I didn't say I was ready to start." -- Smile and say: "My bad, I jumped ahead because I'm excited about what we put together. What questions do you still have?"

---

## Close #2: The Free Mockup Close

**When to use:** On the discovery call when you can tell they're interested but not ready to commit on the spot. This is your bread and butter for a new agency.

**How it works:** Offer to build a free mockup that's SO good they can't say no.

> "Tell you what, [Name]. I don't want you to take my word for it -- I want to
> show you. Let me build a custom mockup of your new homepage. Your branding, your
> photos, your info. Completely free, zero obligation.
>
> I'll have it for you in 48 hours. If you love it, we'll talk about making it
> live. If you hate it, we'll shake hands and part friends. Fair enough?"

**The psychology:** Once they see their business on a beautiful, professional website with their name on it, they feel ownership. It's not "a website" anymore -- it's "MY website." That emotional attachment is incredibly powerful. Studies show that once someone visualizes owning something, they're 3x more likely to buy.

**The key:** The mockup has to be GOOD. Not a template. Not a wireframe. A real, polished homepage that looks like the finished product. Spend 2-3 hours on it. This is your highest-leverage sales activity.

---

## Close #3: The ROI Close

**When to use:** When price is the main concern, or when you're selling a higher-tier package ($999-$2,499).

**How it works:** Break the price down to a per-customer cost and compare it to their revenue per customer.

> "Let's do some quick math together, [Name].
>
> Your average customer spends about $[X] per visit, right? And a loyal customer
> comes in maybe [X times per month/year]?
>
> So one new loyal customer is worth roughly $[annual value] to your business.
>
> The $[price] website investment needs to bring you just [calculate] new customers
> to pay for itself completely. After that, every new customer it brings in is
> pure profit.
>
> For context, a well-optimized local business website typically generates 10-30
> new customer inquiries per month. Even if we're conservative and say only 10%
> of those become actual customers, that's 1-3 new customers every single month
> from a one-time investment.
>
> Over 12 months, that's $[calculate big number] in new revenue from a
> $[price] investment.
>
> Does that math work for you?"

**Tip:** Write this out on paper or on screen during the call. Visual math is more persuasive than verbal math. Put the numbers right in the proposal.

---

## Close #4: The Urgency Close

**When to use:** When the prospect agrees with the value but keeps stalling. Use with care -- false urgency destroys trust.

**How it works:** Create real, legitimate scarcity.

> "[Name], I want to be transparent with you. I'm a one-man operation by design --
> that's how I deliver the quality I deliver. But it also means I can only take on
> 3-4 projects at a time. Right now I've got [X] slots open for this month, and
> I've got two other Heights businesses I'm talking to this week.
>
> I'm not saying this to pressure you -- I genuinely want to work with [Business Name]
> and I'd rather give you first priority since we've already done the mockup work.
> But I also don't want you to come back in two weeks and hear me say I'm booked
> out for a month.
>
> If we lock in the deposit today, I can start your project this week. Does that
> work for you?"

**IMPORTANT:** This only works if it's TRUE. If you have zero other clients, do not say you have two. Houston is a small community and word travels. Instead, use a different version:

> "Here's my honest situation -- I'm building my client roster right now, which
> means the businesses that get in early get more of my time and attention than
> the ones who come later when I'm juggling multiple projects. Right now, you'd
> get the white-glove treatment. I can't promise that'll be the case in a few
> months. Want to take advantage of that?"

---

## Close #5: The Guarantee Close

**When to use:** When the prospect is risk-averse or has been burned before. This is your closer of last resort.

**How it works:** Remove all the risk from their side of the equation.

> "I'll tell you what, [Name]. I'm going to make this the easiest decision you've
> ever made.
>
> Here's my guarantee: if you don't absolutely LOVE the final website -- if it's not
> a dramatic improvement over what you have now -- you don't pay the final balance.
> I'll eat the cost and you can keep whatever work we've done.
>
> I've never had anyone take me up on that, because I don't deliver anything less
> than great. But I want you to know that the risk is entirely on me, not on you.
>
> With that on the table, is there any reason not to get started today?"

**Structure the guarantee carefully:**
- 50% deposit upfront (non-refundable -- covers your time/costs)
- 50% due at delivery
- The guarantee applies to the final 50%
- "Satisfaction" means completing one round of revisions -- they don't get unlimited free work

This way your max downside is 50% of the deal, and in practice, nobody ever invokes this because you do great work and the mockup already set expectations.

# 6. UPSELL FRAMEWORK

## The Ladder: $499 --> $999 --> $2,499

### How It Works

Never try to upsell during the first sale. Land the initial deal, deliver an incredible result, and THEN expand.

### The 3-Week Upsell Window

---

> "Hey [Name], it's Donny. I wanted to check in on how the new site is performing.
> I've been keeping an eye on the analytics and I've got some cool stuff to share
> with you. Got 10 minutes?"

On the call, share:
- Google Analytics data (visitors, page views, time on site)
- Any Google Search Console improvements
- Google Business Profile views/clicks if you optimized it
- Specific wins: "You had 47 people visit your menu page this week."

After sharing the good news:

> "The site is working great and I'm pumped about these early numbers. But I'll

>
> Can I tell you about two things that would take this to the next level?"

# 7. REFERRAL SYSTEM

## The "Happy Client Machine"

Referrals are the highest-converting, lowest-cost leads you will ever get. A referred lead closes at 3-5x the rate of a cold lead and costs you exactly $0 to acquire.

### When to Ask

Ask at the **peak of excitement**, which is one of two moments:
1. The day the website goes live (they're showing everyone)
2. The moment they tell you about a result ("Donny, I got 3 calls from the site this week!")

NEVER ask for a referral before delivering results. Earn it first.

---

### The Ask (Word for Word)

**Version 1: The Direct Ask (use at site launch)**

> "[Name], I'm so glad you love how this turned out. Can I ask you a quick favor?
>
> You know other business owners in the Heights. If you know anyone who's in the
> same boat you were in -- needing a better website or wanting to show up higher
> on Google -- I'd really appreciate an introduction. I'll take just as good care
> of them as I took care of you.
>
> Is there anyone who comes to mind right off the top of your head?"

**Version 2: The Incentive Ask (use at the results check-in)**

> "[Name], your site has been killing it and I love seeing these numbers.
>
> I wanted to let you know about something I'm doing for my best clients. For
> every business owner you refer to me who signs up, I'll give you a $100 credit
> toward your next project -- whether that's updates to your site, additional
> pages, whatever you need.
>
> And for the person you refer, I'll give them 10% off their first project as a
> thank-you.
>
> Everybody wins. Do you know a couple people I should talk to?"

**Version 3: The Passive Ask (use via email after launch)**

> Subject: Quick thank you + a favor
>
> Hey [Name],
>
> Just wanted to say thanks again for trusting me with [Business Name]'s website.
> Seeing it live and performing well is the best part of my job.
>
> If you know any other business owners in the Heights who could use a similar
> upgrade, I'd love an intro. Here's a simple way to do it -- just forward this
> email to them with a quick "hey, this is the guy I told you about" and I'll
> take it from there.
